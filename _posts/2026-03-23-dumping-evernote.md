I finally broke up with Evernote. Thirteen years. That's longer than some marriages.

## The Good Years

I started using Evernote around 2013, back when it felt like magic. Scan a receipt? Searchable. Clip a web page? Saved forever. I threw everything in there: tax documents, medical records, car paperwork, journal entries, guitar tabs, shopping lists, random 3 AM thoughts I was sure would change my life. Nearly 2,000 notes over the years. It was my second brain, and for a long time, it was a good one.

I paid for Premium happily. It earned every dollar.

## The Slow Decline

It's hard to pinpoint when things went south. There wasn't a single moment. It was more like watching a friend slowly stop trying. The app got heavier. The price kept climbing. New features appeared that nobody asked for while old annoyances never got fixed. The mobile app, once snappy, started feeling like it was running through mud.

Every year the renewal email showed up with a bigger number attached to a product that felt like less. At some point I realized I was paying out of loyalty, not value.

The final straw was mundane. I opened the app, waited for it to sync, stared at the price tag, and thought: I don't need this anymore.

## WebSidian

Instead of jumping to the next shiny notes app and setting myself up for this exact conversation again in 2031, I built my own. I call it WebSidian. It's a simple web app that looks and feels like Obsidian, syncs to Google Drive, and runs on my own machine.

I exported all 1,950 notes out of Evernote, converted them to plain Markdown files, and imported them. The migration was messy (Evernote's export format is... creative), but it worked. Every note I've ever written is now a text file I own. No subscription. No lock-in. No app that needs permission to access my own thoughts.

There's something satisfying about that. My notes from 2013 will still be readable in 2043, because they're just text files in a folder.

## Thirteen Years

I don't regret using Evernote. For most of that run, it was exactly what I needed. But thirteen years taught me something: the only note-taking system that lasts is one where you own the files. Everything else is a rental.

So long, Evernote. It was a good run.
